1.	Introduction 
1.1.	Historical Context and the Role of Histopathology
Histology is the microscopic study of the structure and composition of biological tissues and has been foundational to medical diagnosis since the nineteenth century [1] [2]. By preparing thin sections of tissue and staining them with dyes that selectively color different cellular components, histologists render the invisible architecture of organs and tissues visible under a microscope [2].
 Histopathology extends this discipline to the study of diseased tissues, serving as the gold standard for diagnosing a wide range of conditions, from infections to malignancies [3] [4]. The word histopathology was first introduced in a book entitled "On the Nature and Structure Characteristic of Cancer" by Johannes Muller in 1838 [4]. When abnormal changes in cell morphology, tissue architecture, or staining patterns are identified, they provide crucial evidence for determining the nature, extent, and severity of disease.
1.2 Cancer Diagnosis and Clinical Challenges
Among the most consequential applications of histopathology is cancer diagnosis and staging. Cancer is the uncontrolled proliferation and spread of abnormal cells, it remains a leading cause of death worldwide [5]. A critical step in determining a patient's prognosis and treatment plan is assessing whether the tumor has metastasized, that is, disseminated beyond its primary site to distant organs and tissues, most commonly through the lymphatic system [6]. Lymph nodes, which function as filters of tissue fluids and as sites of lymphocyte production, are therefore routinely examined histopathologically to detect metastatic deposits [7]. In breast cancer, the histopathological assessment of sentinel lymph node biopsies directly underpins the TNM staging system and informs treatment decisions [8]. However, manual examination of whole-slide images is time-consuming, subjective, and prone to inter-observer variability for instance, breast cancer subtyping by expert pathologists has shown accuracy rates as low as 57.08%, and prostate cancer grading inter-observer agreement has been reported at only 57.90%[9].
1.3 Tools and Techniques in Microscopy and Staining
This histopathological examination relies fundamentally on microscopy and tissue staining. The light microscope, first assembled by Zacharias Jansen in the sixteenth century and perfected by Leeuwenhoek in 1673, remains the primary tool for routine diagnostic work, with resolution governed by the ratio between the wavelength of light and the width of the diaphragm opening [4]. More advanced instruments, including scanning electron microscopes (SEM), transmission electron microscopes (TEM), cryo-electron microscopes (Cryo-EM), fluorescence microscopes, and phase-contrast microscopes, extend resolution to the ultrastructural and even atomic level for specialized applications [4]. 
The most widely used stain in histopathology is hematoxylin and eosin (H&E), first described in 1875–1878 , in which hematoxylin colors cell nuclei blue and eosin stains the cytoplasm and extracellular matrix pink, enabling pathologists to evaluate tissue architecture and cellular morphology[1] [10]. Beyond H&E, a range of special histochemical stains exist for specific purposes: the periodic acid-Schiff (PAS) stain for polysaccharides and fungi, the Ziehl-Neelsen stain for acid-fast bacilli, Masson's trichrome for connective tissue, the Grocott-Gomori methenamine silver (GMS) stain for fungal organisms, and Gram stain for bacteria, among others [2] [11].
 Immunohistochemistry (IHC) further advances diagnostic capability by using labelled antibodies to detect specific antigens within tissue sections, enabling the identification of particular cell types, tumor markers, and prognostic biomarkers such as HER2 amplification in breast cancer [12], [13]. However, the preparation of high-quality slides is itself a complex, multi-step process — involving fixation, processing, embedding, sectioning, staining, and mounting — each step of which can introduce artefacts that alter tissue morphology and potentially compromise diagnostic accuracy [3].
1.4 Digital Pathology and Computational Challenges
The transition from optical microscopy to digital pathology has fundamentally transformed this landscape. Whole-slide imaging (WSI) technology enables the digitization of glass slides at high resolution (e.g., 240 nanometers per pixel), producing multi-gigapixel images that can be viewed, shared, and analyzed computationally [14], [15]. However, the sheer size of WSIs- the CAMELYON dataset alone, comprising 1,399 WSIs, consumes roughly 2.95 terabytes of storage [16], means they cannot be loaded into standard GPU memory in their entirety. This has given rise to patch-based processing paradigms, in which WSIs are tiled into small patches (typically 96×96 or 256×256 pixels), classified individually, and then aggregated into slide-level or patient-level predictions[17], [18]. 
Pre-processing steps: including tissue detection, background removal, and stain normalization, are essential to ensure that patches fed into computational models are of sufficient quality [19]. Stain colour variation across institutions, arising from differences in staining protocols, reagent brands, tissue preparation, and scanner hardware, degrades model performance on unseen institutions which is a problem known as domain shift. 
Several classical stain normalization methods were proposed by Macenko [20],[21], decompose H&E images into stain vectors and map them to a reference template. It was found in a comprehensive study across nine laboratories and four organs that extensive stain augmentation in H&E colour space was more effective than normalization alone for CNN generalization [22]. A systematic review  was provided in Information Fusion grouping methods into four categories and concluding that structure-preserving unified transformation-based methods consistently outperform alternatives [23]. More advanced approaches include GAN-based methods[24] and domain adaptation techniques like AIDA [25].
1.5 From Machine Learning to Deep Learning Foundation Models
Classical machine learning approaches to histopathology image analysis relied on manually engineered features, such as Local Binary Patterns (LBP), Scale-Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT), and Grey-Level Co-occurrence Matrices (GLCM)  which are classified using Support Vector Machines (SVMs), Random Forests, and similar algorithms [26]. While these methods demonstrated the feasibility of automated detection across several cancer types (breast, colorectal, prostate, gastric), they were heavily dependent on human intuition for feature selection and struggled to capture the full heterogeneity of tumor microenvironments [27].
 Conventional ML was directly compared with deep learning on breast cancer histopathology, finding that deep learning approaches achieved accuracies of 94–98% for binary classification versus 85–89% for classical methods, though classical approaches retained a competitive edge in data-limited settings[28] . The advent of deep learning, and specifically convolutional neural networks (CNNs), marked a decisive shift: models could now learn hierarchical feature representations directly from raw pixel data, eliminating the need for handcrafted feature engineering [29]. 
In a landmark JAMA study, it demonstrated through the CAMELYON16 challenge that the best deep learning algorithm achieved an AUC of 0.994 for slide-level lymph node metastasis classification, significantly outperforming a panel of eleven pathologists working under time constraints (mean AUC 0.810) [8]. Transfer learning from ImageNet-pretrained architectures further accelerated progress; where it was shown that fine-tuned VGG16 yielded 92.60% accuracy versus substantially lower performance when trained from scratch  [30], and it wast demonstrated that even CNNs pre-trained on histopathology from other tissue types outperformed training from scratch [31]. 
More recently, self-supervised learning and pathology-specific foundation models, including CTransPath [32], UNI  [33], CONCH [34], GigaPath[35], and the Virchow family [36], [37], pretrained on millions of whole-slide images, have pushed the state of the art further, achieving strong performance across diverse tasks with minimal labelled data. Independent benchmarking across 13 cohorts found CONCH and Virchow2 to be the top-performing models[38]. At the slide level, Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) frameworks aggregate patch-level features into whole-slide predictions, enabling clinically relevant classification without exhaustive pixel-level annotation[39], [40]. A recent systematic review covering 73 studies found that 86% of computational lymph node analysis work focused on metastasis identification, with multi-scale models mimicking multiple microscopy zooms showing the most promise[41].
1.6 Identified Gaps and Research Objectives
Despite this considerable progress, several gaps remain. The interaction between preprocessing pipelines and modern foundation model features is largely untested, whether models pretrained on millions of diverse slides already encode stain-invariant features, rendering explicit normalization redundant, is an open question. 
Isolated tumor cells (ITCs) in lymph nodes remain exceedingly difficult to detect automatically, with reported detection rates below 40% [42]. Most published work focuses on H&E stains, and generalization to other staining protocols is underexplored. The computational cost of the largest foundation models limits accessibility in resource-constrained settings, though models trained on far fewer WSIs show that it can match the performance of Virchow2 [43]. 
Finally, the optimal combination of adaptive preprocessing, domain-specific augmentation, and foundation model transfer learning for robust cross-center generalizability has not been systematically investigated, a gap that the present work aims to address.
 2. Literature Review
 2.1 The Gigapixel Challenge and the Camelyon Benchmarks 
The transition from traditional microscopy to digital pathology introduced a fundamental computational challenge. A single whole-slide image (WSI) digitized at high resolution (e.g., 240 nanometers per pixel) is a multi-gigapixel image that cannot be loaded into standard GPU memory in its entirety [44].  As it was detailed, the CAMELYON dataset alone, comprising 1,399 WSIs from breast cancer patients across multiple centers, consumes roughly 2.95 terabytes of storage [16].  Because processing these images whole is computationally infeasible, researchers developed patch-based processing paradigms: WSIs are tiled into small patches (typically 96×96 or 256×256 pixels), classified individually, and aggregated into slide-level or patient-level predictions. 
Breast cancer staging depends critically on histopathological assessment of sentinel lymph node biopsies, where pathologists examine H&E stained tissue sections for metastatic deposits. This process underpins the TNM classification system and directly informs treatment decisions. However, manual examination of WSIs is time-consuming, subjective, and prone to inter-observer variability, for example, breast cancer subtyping by expert pathologists has shown accuracy rates as low as 57.08%, and prostate cancer grading inter-observer agreement has been reported at only 57.90% [9].
The Camelyon Grand Challenges were established to catalyze automated solutions. The CAMELYON16 challenge provided 399 WSIs from two Dutch medical centers. In a landmark JAMA study, it was reported that the best deep learning algorithm achieved an AUC of 0.994 for slide-level classification, significantly outperforming 11 pathologists under simulated time constraints (mean AUC 0.810) and matching an expert pathologist working without time constraints[8]. Concurrently a CNN framework was developed and detected 92.4% of tumors at 8 false positives per image, compared to 82.7% for the previous best automated approach and 73.2% for exhaustive human search; they also discovered two mislabeled training slides [45].
The CAMELYON17 challenge extended the task to patient-level pN-staging across five centers.  Outcomes in IEEE Transactions on Medical Imaging were summarized: the best algorithms achieved a weighted Cohen's kappa of 0.89, but all participants struggled with ITCs (detection rates below 40%). The best results used pre-trained architectures such as ResNet, and simple ensembles of top algorithms reached kappa of 0.93.  To enable patch-level research, Veeling et al. (2018) introduced PatchCamelyon (PCam): 327,680 patches of 96×96 pixels extracted from Camelyon16 slides, labeled for metastatic tissue. PCam has since become one of the most widely used histopathology benchmarks[42]. 
More recently, 1,399 WSIs from both Camelyon datasets were systematically reprocessed, which included removing low-quality slides, correcting erroneous labels, and providing expert pixel annotations for the previously unreleased test set. They upgraded the binary task to a four-class problem (negative, micro-metastasis, macro-metastasis, ITC) and re-evaluated modern foundation models and MIL methods on the cleaned data[16].
 2.2 Preprocessing: Stain Normalization and Color Augmentation 
A fundamental challenge in computational pathology, not only in breast cancer metastasis detection but across organ types and cancer subtypes,  is that H&E stained slides exhibit substantial color variation across institutions, arising from differences in staining protocols, reagent brands, tissue preparation, section thickness, and scanner hardware. This variation degrades model performance on unseen institutions, a problem known as domain shift. 
Classical stain normalization methods The Macenko method works by decomposing H&E images into stain vectors via singular value decomposition in optical density space, then mapping to a reference template [20]. This method remains widely used for its simplicity. The Vahadane method extended this using sparse non-negative matrix factorization, which better preserves tissue morphology. Comparative evaluations show Vahadane achieves higher structural fidelity (SSIM), while Macenko provides more consistent chromatic distributions [21].  At the whole-slide level, WSICS is a fully automated algorithm that aligns chromatic and density distributions per stain component to a template WSI, achieving the lowest color variation across 125 WSIs from 5 laboratories [46].  A comprehensive review in Information Fusion, grouping methods into four categories, showed that structure-preserving unified transformation-based methods consistently outperform alternatives [23].
Quantifying the impact: Stain augmentation, normalization, and a neural network-based normalization were compared on heterogeneous data from 9 laboratories and 4 organs. Their key finding was that extensive stain augmentation in H&E color space was more effective than normalization alone for CNN generalization [22]. Augmentation directly in H&E color space (decomposing via color deconvolution, modifying H and E channels stochastically, then projecting back to RGB) significantly improved robustness to unseen stain variations [47]. 
Normalization on Camelyon and PCam: Six configurations combining Macenko and Vahadane normalization with color augmentation on PCam were compared. Macenko combined with color augmentation improved accuracy by 1.59% and F1 by 2.50% over baseline, modest gains reflecting PCam's relatively homogeneous origin, but consistent [48]. CAGAN is a color adaptive generative network combining supervised and unsupervised learning, reporting 5–10% improvement on CAMELYON16, CAMELYON17, and other datasets over baselines without normalization [49].  Bayesian blind color deconvolution improved AUC from 0.9491 to 0.9656 on Camelyon-16 and from 0.9279 to 0.9541 on Camelyon-17 [50].  Color normalization improved retrieval accuracy to 97% on CAMELYON17, a 14% increase [51].  Aggregating predictions from the two best normalization methods with augmentation improved external-set AUC from 0.860 to 0.892, confirming preprocessing benefits are most pronounced when generalizing across institutions [52]. 
GAN-based and learned approaches: HistAuGAN is a structure-preserving multi-domain stain augmentation using disentangled representations. When evaluated on Camelyon17, it outperformed conventional color augmentation and mitigated batch effects [24].  Learnable preprocessing operations trainable end-to-end with classification networks were also proposed [53]. Domain-specific modifications to RandAugment for H&E achieved AUC of 0.964 on Camelyon17 versus 0.958 for manual augmentation[54], [55].
Domain adaptation beyond color: AIDA (Adversarial Fourier-based Domain Adaptation) was proposed, which significantly outperformed both color normalization and standard adversarial approaches across four cancer types from multiple centers, suggesting stain normalization alone is insufficient for full cross-center generalization [25]. It was similarly found that adversarial training yielded significant improvements over color-normalized approaches.  Additionally, combining stain normalization with segmentation-driven patch extraction was shown to improve accuracy by 17–18% for prostate and breast cancer detection [57]. 
 
Gaps: Most normalization methods require selecting a reference image, introducing subjectivity. GAN-based methods risk mode collapse or information loss. The interaction between normalization and modern foundation models remains underexplored , whether models pretrained on millions of diverse slides already encode stain-invariant features, rendering explicit normalization redundant, is an open question. 
  2.3 Model Architectures: From Handcrafted Features to Deep Learning 
The pre-deep learning era: Before deep learning, computational pathology relied on traditional machine learning, where researchers manually extracted morphological and texture features, such as Local Binary Patterns (LBP), Scale-Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT), and Grey-Level Co-occurrence Matrices (GLCM), and classified them using Support Vector Machines (SVMs), Random Forests, and other algorithms[26]. While these methods demonstrated that automated detection was possible across various cancer types (breast, colorectal, prostate, gastric), they were heavily dependent on human intuition for feature selection and struggled to capture the immense heterogeneity of tumor microenvironments[58]. This limitation motivated the decisive shift toward deep learning. 
 
Early CNN approaches: With the emergence of CNNs, models could automatically learn hierarchical features directly from raw pixel data, eliminating the need for handcrafted feature engineering. On PatchCamelyon, Aparicio et al. (2021) built a modified VGG16 achieving AUCs of 0.91/0.90 (validation/test)[59]. A hybrid model ensembling DenseNet169 and ResNet50 for feature extraction was proposed , achieving an AUC of 0.971 and accuracy of 0.982 on PCam , they noted that DenseNet's dense connectivity patterns effectively alleviated the vanishing gradient problem inherent in very deep networks.   A semi-supervised learning with pseudo-labels on PCam was demonstrated, significantly outperforming CNN baselines[60].  A metric learning with contrastive losses was applied, reaching 90.36% accuracy, then state-of-the-art [61]. ESRGAN, adaptive NSCT, and multi-attention CNNs were combined with wavelet transforms, achieving 93.67% accuracy and 94% AUC on PCam[62]. Beyond breast cancer, HistopathAI was developed, which is  a novel framework combining supervised contrastive learning with hybrid deep feature fusion from EfficientNetB3 and ResNet50, achieving state-of-the-art performance across seven public histopathology datasets spanning multiple cancer types demonstrating the generalizability challenge across domains[9]. 
Addressing structural limitations of CNNs: As CNNs became standard, researchers identified domain-specific limitations. Histopathology images exhibit inherent rotational and reflectional symmetries that natural images do not, tissue appears equally valid at any orientation. Standard CNNs waste parameter capacity learning these variations redundantly. P4M-DenseNet, a rotation-equivariant CNN that explicitly encodes these symmetries using group equivariant convolutions, addressed this issue. This resulted in vastly more stable predictions under input rotation and improved tumor detection on Camelyon16. The PCam benchmark itself was introduced alongside this model [63]. This idea was further extended with Dense Steerable Filter CNNs, achieving state-of-the-art on breast tumor classification, colon gland segmentation, and nuclear segmentation with significantly fewer parameters [64].
Second, CNNs often lacked global spatial context when analyzing isolated patches. MMSEN (Multi-scale Multi-head Self-attention Ensemble Network) addressed this, as it integrates Convolutional Block Attention Modules (CBAM) and self-attention to guide the model toward critical tumor-related features. On PCam, MMSEN achieved a ROC-AUC of 99.01%, F1-score of 98.00%, and MCC of 96.00% [44].
Transfer learning from ImageNet: A pivotal shift came with ImageNet-pretrained networks. Despite the large visual domain gap between natural photographs and tissue microscopy, ImageNet features proved surprisingly effective. Researchers across breast, colorectal, and gastric cancer domains embraced transfer learning by fine-tuning architectures like AlexNet, VGG, and ResNet on histopathology data [65]. When Inception-V3 and ResNet50 were fine-tuned; ResNet50 achieved 97.50% accuracy on four breast histology subtypes [66]. When training from scratch was compared with transfer learning with VGG16, VGG19, and ResNet50, it was found that fine-tuned VGG16 yielded 92.60% accuracy and 95.65% AUC, substantially outperforming networks trained from scratch [67]. Even CNNs pre-trained on histopathology from other tissue types outperformed training from scratch (AUC 0.918 vs. 0.867) [68].
The domain gap problem: While ImageNet transfer learning consistently improved over training from scratch, the visual dissimilarity between natural images and histopathology was a persistent concern. Domain-specific versus ImageNet weights across three downstream tasks including both H&E and IHC stains were compared, finding domain-specific pretrained weights yielded higher performance, lower training time, and better feature reuse [69]. This foreshadowed the shift toward domain-specific pretraining. 
2.4 Self-Supervised Learning and Pathology Foundation Models 
The scarcity of labeled histopathology data, where annotation requires expert pathologists, motivated self-supervised learning (SSL) approaches that could leverage the vast quantities of unlabeled WSIs available across institutions. This "annotation bottleneck" was a persistent limitation of fully supervised approaches. The power of SSL for clinical applications was demonstrated by utilizing the Bootstrap Your Own Latent (BYOL) framework to accurately identify eyelid malignant melanoma using only a fraction of the labeled data previously required, confirming that self-supervised features transfer effectively to rare pathology tasks [70].
Early SSL: when contrastive self-supervised learning across 60 histopathology datasets without labels is applied, it was found that histopathology-pretrained networks outperformed ImageNet-pretrained ones by up to 7.5% in accuracy and 8.9% in F1 [71].
CTransPath: CTransPath is a hybrid CNN-Transformer backbone pretrained using semantically-relevant contrastive learning (SRCL). It achieved state-of-the-art on nine public datasets spanning five task types, outperforming both ImageNet-supervised and self-supervised pretraining [72].
UNI: UNI is pretrained on over 100 million images from 100,000+ diagnostic WSIs across 20 tissue types. Evaluated on 34 tasks, UNI outperformed prior state-of-the-art and demonstrated resolution-agnostic classification and few-shot slide classification [73].
CONCH: CONCH is a visual-language foundation model trained on over 1.17 million image-caption pairs. CONCH achieved state-of-the-art on 14 diverse benchmarks spanning classification, segmentation, captioning, and retrieval [74].
GigaPath: Prov-GigaPath is a whole-slide pathology foundation model pretrained on 1.3 billion 256×256 pathology image tiles from 171,189 WSIs covering 31 tissue types. Using a novel vision transformer architecture adapted from LongNet for ultra-large-context modeling, GigaPath achieved state-of-the-art on 25 out of 26 tasks in their benchmark [75].
Virchow: Virchow is trained on 1.5 million WSIs from 100,000 patients. It achieved 0.95 specimen-level AUC for pan-cancer detection across nine common and seven rare cancers, and showed that with limited labeled data, Virchow-based detectors matched tissue-specific clinical-grade models [76].
Virchow2: Virchow2 (632M parameters), Virchow2G (1.9B), and a distilled 22M variant, trained on 3.1 million WSIs. They achieved state-of-the-art on 12 tile-level tasks and showed that combining domain-specific methods, data scale, and model scale yields the best performance [77].
2.5 Independent Benchmarking of Foundation Models 
The rapid proliferation of foundation models created urgent need for independent evaluation. When 19 models across 13 cohorts (6,818 patients, 9,528 slides) were benchmarked, CONCH yielded the highest performance, Virchow2 close second. An ensemble of CONCH and Virchow2 outperformed individual models in 55% of tasks [78]. Furthermore, 31 models across 41 tasks were benchmarked, finding Virchow2 highest overall and that model/data size did not consistently correlate with performance [78]. PathBench (presented across 15,888 WSIs from 10 hospitals) found Virchow2 and H-Optimus-1 the most effective [79]. 
2.6 Multiple Instance Learning for Whole-Slide Prediction 
While patch-level classification is the building block, clinical decisions require slide-level or patient-level predictions. MIL treats each slide as a "bag" of patch instances. A proposed MIL-CNN framework achieved up to 89.52% accuracy for breast tumors [80]. A Prototypical MIL using unsupervised clustering achieved AUC of 88.2% on Camelyon16 and outperforming existing MIL methods for micro-metastases [81]. Modern pipelines now use foundation model features (from UNI, GigaPath, CTransPath, etc.) fed into advanced MIL aggregators such as ABMIL, CLAM, or TransMIL to generate slide-level predictions [82].
2.7 Gaps, Limitations, and Future Directions 
Despite considerable progress, several gaps remain. The interaction between preprocessing pipelines and foundation model features is largely untested, whether explicit normalization adds benefit when using models pretrained on millions of diverse slides is an open question. Independent benchmarks may suffer from data leakage with public datasets. ITCs remain the most challenging subtype (detection below 40%). The computational cost of the largest models limits accessibility, though models trained on far fewer WSIs can match Virchow2 [83]. Most work focuses on H&E stains; generalization to other protocols is underexplored. Finally, the optimal combination of adaptive preprocessing, domain-specific augmentation, and foundation model transfer learning for cross-center generalizability, the central aim of the present work, has not been systematically investigated.
